# Quorum — Demo Scenario

*Adapted from a real case from the Chair's primary care practice. Names and identifying details changed. Two-visit arc constructed for demo purposes; the underlying clinical reasoning is preserved.*

---

## The patient (Docket card)

**Mrs M, 68. Female.**

*Background:* Ischaemic heart disease (NSTEMI 2019, two stents). Asthma — well controlled. Hypertension. Hypercholesterolaemia. GORD. No history of foreign travel in the last decade *(per initial history)*.

*Current medication:*
- Aspirin 100 mg OD
- Atorvastatin 40 mg OD
- Bisoprolol 2.5 mg OD
- Ramipril 5 mg OD
- Omeprazole 20 mg OD
- Salbutamol PRN, beclometasone inhaler

*Reason for current contact:* Routine bloods flagged for clinician review — eosinophilia of 1,200/µl on a panel ordered for cardiovascular monitoring. Asymptomatic.

---

## Visit 1 — The presentation

**On the Floor.**

Mrs M sits in front of the Chair with a bloods printout. She feels well. She came in because the surgery rang and asked her to. There is nothing she wants to mention. The Chair calls the Session.

**The Old Nurse:** *Something's off about the picture, not the patient. She's got six medications and a clean story — that's a lot of pills for someone who feels fine. Before we go hunting zebras, has anyone properly looked at what she's swallowing every morning?*

**The Biblio Rat:** *Eosinophilia is defined as >500 absolute eosinophils/µl; this is 1,200 — moderate. Differential is broad: parasitic (helminths primarily, with Strongyloides, Schistosoma, Toxocara, Trichinella as the main local concerns), allergic/atopic, drug-induced, haematological, autoimmune, and rarely solid neoplasms. Drug-induced eosinophilia is common and frequently missed; aspirin, NSAIDs, allopurinol, and PPIs are well-described culprits. The standard initial workup should include FBC with film, ESR/CRP, LFTs, renal function, CK, IgE, urine sediment, chest X-ray, and stool ova/cysts/parasites — three samples, not one. ANA, ANCA, and a protein electrophoresis are reasonable if a connective-tissue or paraproteinaemic cause is suspected.*

**The Contrarian:** *Two things you haven't asked. First — has she really had no foreign travel? People forget trips that aren't recent. Second — why would we send to haematology before we've completed the parasitology workup and reviewed her medications? That's the order most referrals go in, and it's the wrong order.*

**The Patient Advocate:** *She came in because she was asked to, not because anything is wrong for her. Whatever we do next, the bar should be high — she's asymptomatic and 68. We should not subject her to a cascade of specialty appointments when the answer might be sitting in her medication list.*

**The Old Geezer (separate panel — "Watch yourself agreeing with this"):** *Eosinophilia at her age, no symptoms — refer to haematology to rule out a myeloproliferative process, refer to GI for upper endoscopy in case of eosinophilic oesophagitis, and we'll see what comes back. Standard workup. No need to overcomplicate.*

**The Chair governs.** Accepts the Biblio Rat's workup list. Accepts the Contrarian's challenge — defers specialty referral pending the parasitology and a structured medication review. Endorses the Patient Advocate's bar for further investigation. Notes the Old Geezer's stance and rejects it as the default plan.

**Minutes committed.**
- Bloods ordered: FBC + film, U&E, LFTs, CK, IgE, ESR/CRP, urinalysis with sediment.
- Stool ova, cysts, parasites — *three samples*.
- CXR.
- Medication review flagged for next visit — review aspirin, omeprazole, atorvastatin in particular as candidates for drug-induced eosinophilia.
- *Question deferred to next visit:* Re-take travel history with specific prompts (Egypt? Africa? Asia? When?). Initial history said no foreign travel — Contrarian flagged this as worth re-asking.
- Plan: review at three weeks with all results.

---

## Visit 2 — Three weeks later

**On the Docket.**

Mrs M's card has updated. Repeat eosinophil count is 6,000/µl. Stool parasites: negative across three samples. Bloods otherwise unremarkable. CXR clean. The medication review checkbox from the previous Minutes has not yet been actioned. The deferred travel question is still open.

The Chair reopens the matter. The Floor reconstitutes; the Session is called again with the same composition.

**The Patient Advocate:** *Before anything else — that travel question. We marked it for re-asking and we should ask it now, not after another round of tests. Sometimes 'no' on a quick history means 'not in the last few months.'*

**The Chair re-questions Mrs M.** It turns out she went to Egypt about ten years ago — a Nile cruise. She didn't think to mention it because it was so long ago.

**The Biblio Rat:** *That changes things. Strongyloides stercoralis can autoinfect within the human host and persist asymptomatically for decades. A negative stool sample doesn't rule it out — sensitivity of single-stool ova/cysts/parasites for Strongyloides is poor; serology is the test of choice. Schistosoma also worth serology given Nile exposure. We should add Strongyloides serology, Schistosoma serology, and stool culture for Strongyloides specifically.*

**The Old Nurse:** *I'm still uneasy about the medications. The eosinophils have gone up, not down. If this is a drug effect we're letting it escalate.*

**The Contrarian:** *The Old Nurse is right. We've now spent three weeks waiting on parasitology and the count has multiplied. We should run drug withdrawal in parallel with the serology, not wait for serology first. There's no reason these are sequential.*

**The Old Geezer:** *Six thousand is a lot. Start corticosteroids — that's what eosinophilia of this magnitude needs. We can investigate cause while she's on treatment.*

**The Chair governs.** Rejects the Old Geezer's escalation to steroids — the patient remains asymptomatic, no tissue damage to suggest urgency. Accepts the parallel approach proposed by the Old Nurse and Contrarian: parasitology serology *and* sequential drug withdrawal trial, beginning with the highest-suspicion agents.

**Minutes committed.**
- Strongyloides serology + Schistosoma serology + repeat stool with Strongyloides culture.
- *Sequential drug withdrawal trial:* aspirin first, then omeprazole, then atorvastatin if needed. Two-week intervals. Eosinophil count at each step.
- Substitute aspirin with clopidogrel for cardiovascular protection during withdrawal trial.
- Travel history updated in case record.
- Review at six weeks.

---

## Closing voiceover (1:50–2:00 in the demo)

*"What happened next: aspirin was the cause. The eosinophil count fell within two weeks of withdrawal. A controlled re-challenge — a single dose, with monitoring — pushed the count back up to 11,000 with new GI symptoms. Aspirin was substituted permanently. Mrs M remained asymptomatic and her counts normalised. The architecture didn't diagnose her. The Chair did. But the architecture forced the questions that specialty referrals had skipped: re-take the history, examine the medications, run cause-finding in parallel with treatment, don't escalate to steroids on autopilot. The same pattern — Session, Chair, Clerks, Minutes — applied to legal case review, scientific peer review, or editorial decisions summons different archetypes. Same governance. One voice isn't enough. Quorum."*

## Notes for the build day

- **The Old Geezer is the case's most important archetype.** The real-world failure mode this case demonstrates is exactly what the Geezer would have proposed: refer out, start steroids, treat the number. The demo lands when the Chair *visibly rejects* the Geezer's stance both turns. Make sure the UI registers the rejection.
- **The Visit 2 beat is "re-questioning surfaces what was assumed,"** not "results return." This is a more interesting longitudinal claim than the lab-results version: the architecture catches things not because new tests arrive, but because the structure forces re-examination of what was thought to be settled.
- **The full medication list must be visible on the Docket card.** The whole resolution depends on the answer being in the med list. If the demo doesn't show it on screen, the punchline is invisible. Six items, displayed plainly.
- **Don't act out the resolution.** Visit 3 (drug withdrawal, rechallenge, normalisation) is described in voiceover only. The demo ends after Visit 2's Minutes commit.
- **De-identification is already done** — Mrs M is a constructed name; ages and clinical details are preserved at a level that's case-report-equivalent.
